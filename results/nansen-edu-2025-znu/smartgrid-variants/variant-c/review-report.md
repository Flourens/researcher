# Review Report: DigiTwin-Energy
## Digital Twins and AI for Flexible Energy Systems Education

**Overall Score:** 5.3/7
**Qualifies for Funding:** YES ✓

## Executive Summary

DigiTwin-Energy is a well-conceived and technically sophisticated proposal that addresses a genuinely critical gap in Ukrainian higher education: the absence of structured, graduate-level instruction in digital twin methodology, physics-informed machine learning, and cybersecurity for energy infrastructure at a moment when Ukraine's energy reconstruction demands precisely these competencies. The proposal's grounding in documented IEA/World Bank damage assessments, the HEREs survey on digital competence gaps, and the Zaporizhzhia RMA reconstruction plan gives it strong evidential credibility. The NTNU-ZNU partnership is substantive and pre-existing, and the 36-month work plan is logically sequenced with realistic SMART targets. All seven mandatory cross-cutting requirements are addressed explicitly and with meaningful specificity, which is above the norm for proposals in this category.

The proposal has several structural weaknesses that prevent it from reaching the 'excellent' (6) threshold. Most significantly, Partner 2 remains unidentified — a critical eligibility gap, since the programme requires a minimum of two confirmed Ukrainian HEIs, and the proposal's division of tasks (WP3 lead), budget allocation (28%), and complementarity rationale all rest on an institution described only as 'in advanced discussions.' This is the single most consequential deficiency and creates legal and operational risk. Additionally, the budget section lacks a detailed line-item breakdown to verify the 29% Norwegian personnel cost claim, exchange costs, and the Living Lab equipment envelope, making cost-effectiveness difficult to assess with precision. The proposal also does not confirm that Partner 2 holds the required bachelor's and master's programme accreditation from Ukrainian authorities.

Despite these weaknesses, the proposal's thematic focus, technical depth, wartime-adapted delivery design, and multi-level sustainability architecture are genuinely impressive. The explicit three-generation staff training model (senior, mid-career, early-career), the physics-informed neural network pedagogy, and the Living Lab's grounding in real Ukrainian energy asset challenges represent substantive innovation beyond anything currently running in the Ukrainian higher education space. With Partner 2 confirmed and the budget detailed, this proposal would be competitive at the 'excellent' level. As submitted, it qualifies for funding — meeting all sub-grade thresholds — but requires pre-contract confirmation of the second Ukrainian partner.

## Criterion Scores

### Relevance of the project (6/7, weight: 30%)

**Sub-scores:**
- 1.1_need: 6/7
- 1.2_results: 6/7
- 1.3_ambition: 6/7
- 1.4_innovation: 6/7
- 1.5_added_value: 5/7

**Strengths:**
- The needs analysis is exceptionally well-evidenced: IEA, World Bank, and HEREs survey data are cited with specific statistics (70% thermal generation damaged, <12% of programmes include DT instruction) that make the case compellingly and are verifiable.
- SMART targets (R1–R7) are clearly defined with baselines, quantitative targets, and timelines — notably the 180-student, 24-staff, 12-exchange figures and the ≥40% gender target — providing a strong accountability framework.
- The four course modules are described at sufficient technical depth (three-layer DT architecture, PINN loss functions, IEC 61850, IEC 62443) to demonstrate genuine expertise and distinguish this from generic 'digital transformation' proposals.
- The wartime-adapted delivery design — downloadable offline materials, Azure local caching, asynchronous assessment — is genuinely context-specific and not boilerplate.
- The framing across four nested target groups (students, staff, industry practitioners, Ukrainian HEI ecosystem) is coherent and reflects a systemic understanding of how skills deficits propagate.
- The ambition level is appropriate: 24 ECTS of new accredited curriculum, OER reaching ≥500 users, ≥5 additional HEIs adopting modules — achievable but genuinely stretching.
- The proposal demonstrates strong knowledge of complementary initiatives (14 Erasmus+ projects, DAAD Ukraine Digital, MSCA) and articulates with specificity why DigiTwin-Energy is not duplicative.

**Weaknesses:**
- The HEREs survey cited (reference [5]) is described as conducted across seven Ukrainian technical universities but lacks a hyperlink or DOI — reviewers cannot independently verify the key statistics (<12% DT instruction, <8% PINN coverage) that underpin the needs argument. If this survey is unpublished or grey literature, its authority is uncertain.
- Partner 2 is unconfirmed, meaning the 180-student target and the geographic diversification of impact are not fully substantiated — approximately half the student pipeline relies on an institution whose accreditation, programme offer, and energy systems expertise have not been documented.
- The 'added value' argument relative to the Nansen programme's other components is asserted but not quantified: the proposal does not identify whether any other Nansen EDU 2025 applicant is targeting energy engineering, meaning the unique positioning claim cannot be externally verified.
- Reference [9] (Zaporizhzhia RMA reconstruction plan) is cited for the 500–800 specialist engineer figure but no URL is provided — in a frontline region, this document's accessibility and authority should be established.
- The proposal notes fewer than 12% of programmes include DT instruction but does not establish a clear causal link between the HEREs survey sample (seven universities) and the specific partner institutions — it is possible ZNU is among the more advanced 12%.

**Recommendations:**
- Confirm Partner 2 before submission and include their accreditation documentation and programme catalogue as an annex; recalculate R2 targets to show how they are distributed between institutions.
- Provide a DOI, URL, or institutional access point for the HEREs survey (reference [5]); if unpublished, quote the full methodology and note that the raw data is available on request.
- Add a brief paragraph confirming that no other Nansen EDU 2025 project known to the applicants targets digital twin methodology for energy engineering in Ukraine, to strengthen the uniqueness claim.
- Include an accessible URL or archival reference for the Zaporizhzhia RMA reconstruction plan (reference [9]) or substitute with a verifiable secondary source citing the 500–800 engineer figure.

### Quality of design and implementation (5/7, weight: 20%)

**Sub-scores:**
- 2.1_implementation_plan: 5/7
- 2.2_risk_assessment: 6/7
- 2.3_cost_effectiveness: 4/7

**Strengths:**
- The five-WP structure is logically sequenced (Foundation → Pilots → Scaling) with clear inter-WP dependencies explicitly described: WP1 outputs feed WP3 delivery; WP4 Living Lab data informs WP2 staff exchanges; WP5 runs concurrently throughout.
- Deliverable numbering (D1.1–D5.5) with specific target months is commendably precise and provides a clear accountability trail.
- The risk assessment covers all eight required categories (security, corruption, climate, gender, human rights, SEAH, data protection, cost-effectiveness) with institution-specific and context-specific mitigation measures rather than generic boilerplate.
- The security risk mitigation is particularly sophisticated: remote-first design, pre-prepared online completion alternatives for exchanges, Azure continuity infrastructure, and a named 5% contingency reserve all reflect genuine operational planning for a conflict environment.
- Anti-corruption measures are unusually detailed: three-quote tendering, dual-confirmation scholarship disbursement, Unit4 accounting system, annual external audit, and a named whistleblower channel — well above the minimum threshold.
- SEAH prevention includes a designated safeguarding lead at NTNU and a named Ukrainian safeguarding contact at each partner institution, with a 72-hour incident reporting commitment to HK-dir — operationally credible.
- The equipment procurement timeline (all purchases by Month 24) explicitly satisfies the 12-month pre-project-end requirement.
- Co-teaching with recorded sessions for future reference is a practical approach that simultaneously develops Ukrainian staff capacity and creates reusable pedagogical assets.

**Weaknesses:**
- No Gantt chart or visual project timeline is included — for a complex 5-WP project, this is a significant omission that makes it harder for reviewers to verify the sequencing and identify resource bottlenecks, particularly around the parallel demands on ZNU personnel in Months 4–18 (WP1, WP2, WP4 all active simultaneously).
- Cost-effectiveness is asserted (EUR 1,200 per student) but no itemised budget table is presented in the proposal. The 29% Norwegian personnel figure cannot be independently verified without seeing the personnel cost lines, and the Living Lab equipment envelope is not costed.
- The pilot cohort in Year 2 targets 60 students but Module 1 (DT Architecture) is being revised until Month 30 — the timeline implies students take an unrevised curriculum in Year 2, which is not acknowledged as a limitation.
- WP3 is led by Partner 2 — an unconfirmed institution — meaning the student training work package has no confirmed lead. This is a structural implementation risk.
- The Industry Pilot Project C (hydropower asset, Year 3) depends on ZNU's Hydropower Professional College and a collaboration with Partner 2 on a 'Dnipro HPP reconstruction context', but the Dnipro HPP is located in Zaporizhzhia/Kherson, a high-security area. No specific risk mitigation addresses data access constraints for this pilot.
- The proposal does not describe a formal change management or amendment process if targets need to be substantially revised — the Steering Committee's role is described but not the threshold conditions under which formal workplan amendments would be submitted to HK-dir.

**Recommendations:**
- Include a Gantt chart (even as a simplified table) covering all WPs, key milestones, and resource allocation by institution — this is effectively mandatory for convincing reviewers of implementation credibility.
- Provide at minimum a summary budget table showing personnel costs by institution, equipment costs, scholarship costs, and travel costs, with the 29% Norwegian cap calculation made explicit.
- Acknowledge the Year 2 pilot cohort's experience with a pre-final curriculum and describe how pilot feedback will be incorporated — framing it as a positive design feature (participatory curriculum refinement) rather than a gap.
- Add a specific mitigation for Pilot C data access risk (Dnipro HPP security constraints) — e.g., use of synthetic or anonymised historical datasets from pre-war periods if live data access is unavailable.
- Define formal amendment thresholds: e.g., if any SMART target cannot be met within 20% of its value, a formal HK-dir-notified workplan amendment is triggered.

### Quality of project group (4/7, weight: 20%)

**Sub-scores:**
- 3.1_complementarity: 5/7
- 3.2_task_division: 4/7
- 3.3_interaction_plan: 5/7

**Strengths:**
- The NTNU personnel profiles are technically credible and well-matched to project tasks: the Project Coordinator's PINN/cyber-physical background aligns with Modules 1-2; the Curriculum Development Lead's OPAL-RT and SCADA expertise aligns with Module 3; the CCIS expert's IEC 62443 background aligns with Module 4.
- ZNU's complementary strengths — HPC simulation, IoT/Azure integration, industrial process modelling — are genuinely distinct from NTNU's strengths and explained clearly, avoiding the 'two institutions doing the same thing' failure mode.
- The three-tier governance structure (PSC, WP Leads, EAB) with specific membership, meeting frequency, and decision-making rules (consensus with casting vote) is more operationally detailed than most proposals at this stage.
- Stakeholder engagement is systematically planned: Ukrenergo regional division, ≥2 DSOs, Ukrainian MES through HEREs network, 15+ HEIs for dissemination webinar — with specific engagement mechanisms (data-sharing agreements, guest lectures, annual seminars) rather than vague 'collaboration.'
- The monthly WP Lead calls and 10-working-day milestone reporting template provide a realistic coordination rhythm for a distributed wartime partnership.
- The inclusion of a rotating student representative on the PSC is a strong governance feature that ensures student voice in project management.

**Weaknesses:**
- Partner 2 is entirely unconfirmed. All key personnel descriptions for Partner 2 are generic placeholders ('Principal Investigator with grid operations research experience, 0.3 FTE'). This means one-third of the consortium is notional — a significant compliance and quality risk given that the programme requires minimum two Ukrainian HEIs with documented accreditation.
- All NTNU personnel names are listed as '[Name TBC]', and the same applies to ZNU personnel. Absent named individuals with verifiable CVs, the expertise claims cannot be validated. The Programme requires a Project Coordinator CV (maximum 4 pages) — this document presumably names the Coordinator, but the proposal as written does not align with that requirement.
- FTE allocations are stated (0.2, 0.15, 0.1 for NTNU; 0.3, 0.25, 0.15 for ZNU) but no calculation is provided to show these are sufficient for the planned workload — particularly ZNU's PI at 0.3 FTE who leads WP4, co-leads WP1, and coordinates staff training under wartime conditions.
- The External Advisory Board is described in principle but no members are named or their institutions specified, making it impossible to assess independence and expertise quality.
- There is no description of conflict-resolution mechanisms beyond the PSC casting vote — for a trilateral partnership operating in high-stress wartime conditions, more granular dispute escalation procedures would strengthen the collaboration architecture.
- SINTEF is mentioned as an informing partner (for Living Lab datasets and industry DT implementations) but has no formal role, no budget allocation, and no Letter of Commitment mentioned. If SINTEF is genuinely contributing, it should be formalised.

**Recommendations:**
- Confirm Partner 2 immediately and provide: institution name, accreditation documentation, programme catalogue confirming bachelor's and master's programmes, named personnel with CVs, and a signed Letter of Commitment before submission.
- Name all key personnel and ensure the Project Coordinator CV (maximum 4 pages) is ready for submission — the proposal's '[Name TBC]' placeholders are not acceptable in a final application.
- Calculate and present a workload justification: show that ZNU's PI at 0.3 FTE can realistically lead WP4, co-lead WP1, coordinate industry partners, and attend PSC/WP meetings — or increase FTE allocation.
- Name at least two prospective EAB members (even as 'intended' subject to their confirmation) to demonstrate that independent review is operationally planned rather than aspirational.
- Clarify SINTEF's role: either formalise it with a Letter of Intent and defined contribution, or remove the references to avoid creating an expectation of SINTEF involvement that the proposal cannot substantiate.
- Add a paragraph on dispute resolution escalation beyond the casting vote mechanism — e.g., referral to NTNU's International Partnerships Office or HK-dir mediation for unresolvable disagreements.

### Project impact (5/7, weight: 30%)

**Sub-scores:**
- 4.1_impact: 6/7
- 4.2_sustainability: 5/7
- 4.3_evaluation: 5/7

**Strengths:**
- The impact narrative is systematically structured across four levels (student, staff, institutional, regional/national) with specific quantitative claims at each level — 180 students, 24 staff, 40 industry practitioners, ≥5 adopting HEIs, 500–900 indirect student reach — rather than vague aspiration.
- The explicit linkage to the Zaporizhzhia RMA reconstruction plan's 500–800 specialist engineer demand makes the workforce impact claim concrete and policy-relevant.
- The four-mechanism sustainability architecture (accreditation, trained faculty cohort, Living Lab as permanent infrastructure, Horizon Europe follow-on) is the most credible sustainability plan seen in this type of programme — it explicitly addresses the three most common failure modes (no accreditation, no trained staff, no infrastructure).
- The three-generation staff development model (senior → mid-career → early-career in successive cohorts) is genuinely innovative and ensures delivery capacity extends 10–15 years post-project — a strong structural sustainability feature.
- The Rector's written commitment to sustain the curriculum and ZNU's commitment to fund Living Lab operational costs (cloud subscription, equipment maintenance) from Year 4 are concrete institutional commitments beyond boilerplate sustainability language.
- The multi-level evaluation framework (student diagnostic/post-module/capstone/graduate tracking; staff pre-post competence; programme SMART targets; institutional baseline/endline) is comprehensive and includes a 12-month post-graduation tracking survey — unusually long-term for a project of this duration.
- The contribution to all three Nansen EDU objectives is clearly articulated and mapped to specific project activities.
- The policy brief pathway to MES adoption as a national curricular track represents a realistic systemic change mechanism, not merely an aspirational claim.

**Weaknesses:**
- The '≥5 additional Ukrainian HEIs adopting modules by Year 3' target is ambitious but the adoption pathway is underspecified: does 'adopting' mean formally integrating into accredited programmes, or merely downloading and using OER materials? These are very different levels of impact and the former requires institutional decision-making processes that rarely complete within a year of OER publication.
- The Horizon Europe follow-on is presented as a Year 3 deliverable on the NTNU-ZNU roadmap, but no specific call, budget envelope, or consortium plan is named. DIGITAL-2027 and ENERGY-2028 are mentioned without verifying that Ukraine is eligible for these specific calls under current Horizon Europe association agreements.
- The graduate tracking survey at 12 months post-graduation is a strong feature, but the proposal does not describe how ZNU will maintain contact with graduates in a high-displacement wartime environment — tracking may be infeasible for students who relocate to other countries.
- The OER target of ≥500 downloads within 12 months of release is not benchmarked against comparable Ukrainian engineering education OER releases — it is unclear whether this is an ambitious or conservative target.
- The impact claim that DigiTwin-Energy will produce a 'nationally significant cohort of digital twin educators' is made without identifying any other programme that might produce competing or complementary educator cohorts, leaving the 'nationally significant' characterisation unvalidated.
- The evaluation methodology does not describe who conducts the institutional baseline assessment (Month 3) and endline assessment (Month 35) — if conducted internally, there is a risk of confirmation bias; if externally, this cost is not visible in the budget.

**Recommendations:**
- Define 'adoption' of OER modules by additional HEIs with a tiered scale: Level 1 (download/use in existing courses), Level 2 (integrate as an elective unit), Level 3 (formally accredited module) — and set realistic targets at each level separately.
- Verify Ukraine's eligibility for the specific Horizon Europe calls cited (DIGITAL-2027, ENERGY-2028) and provide a one-paragraph follow-on funding roadmap with named call identifiers and estimated submission dates.
- Add a displacement-resilient graduate tracking protocol: e.g., use LinkedIn professional networks, ZNU alumni association database, and partner industry contacts rather than institutional email addresses, which may become inactive post-graduation.
- Budget for an external evaluator to conduct the institutional baseline (Month 3) and endline (Month 35) assessments, and name this cost line explicitly in the budget — this strengthens the credibility of impact claims and satisfies good practice in educational evaluation.
- Benchmark the ≥500 OER download target against comparable Ukrainian HEI OER releases (e.g., via the Ukrainian Open Education Platform analytics) to justify whether this is appropriately ambitious.

## Cross-Cutting Compliance

- **antiCorruption:** ✓ — Strong. The proposal goes well beyond the minimum: three-quote competitive tendering above NOK 100,000, dual-confirmation scholarship disbursement (academic registrar + supervisor), Unit4 accounting system auditable by HK-dir, annual external financial audit (budgeted), mandatory anti-corruption training for Ukrainian partners before project start, and a named whistleblower channel via NTNU's ethics office. This is among the most operationally specific anti-corruption sections reviewers are likely to encounter.
- **climate:** ✓ — Well-addressed with four specific measures: digital-first meeting policy, rail preference for physical travel, certified CO₂ offset for unavoidable air travel, and use of Microsoft's carbon-neutral datacentre infrastructure for Azure-hosted simulations. The thematic contribution to grid optimisation and renewable integration is correctly identified as a positive environmental externality. Minor gap: no quantified carbon footprint estimate or target reduction percentage is provided.
- **gender:** ✓ — Strong and operationalised. Binding ≥40% female target for students (including exchanges), ≥40% female PSC membership, gender-neutral curriculum language, diverse role models in case studies, flexible assessment accommodating caregiving, and gender-disaggregated annual reporting. The specific acknowledgment of wartime caregiving burden on women and the reference to ZNU's ESG strategy demonstrate contextual sophistication. Missing: no data on current female enrolment in energy engineering at partner institutions to establish baseline.
- **humanRights:** ✓ — Adequately addressed with specific attention to the unique risks of a conflict-affected context: voluntary participation without location disclosure, no IP-location data on LMS, academic freedom from government pre-approval, and inclusion of students from occupied/contested territories where technically feasible. The export control review of cybersecurity content against EU Council Regulation 2021/821 is a technically credible and specific measure. Could be strengthened by explicitly addressing accessibility for students with disabilities.
- **seah:** ✓ — Well-addressed with a seven-point framework: mandatory training for all staff, signed Code of Conduct, confidential reporting mechanism through NTNU's student welfare office, exchange-specific induction on rights and reporting, designated safeguarding lead at NTNU and contact at each partner institution, structured supervision protocols, and 72-hour incident reporting commitment to HK-dir. This is operationally credible and commensurate with NTNU's institutional SEAH framework.
- **dataProtection:** ✓ — Adequately addressed: GDPR compliance under NTNU as data controller, student data on NTNU's GDPR-compliant infrastructure only, energy asset data anonymised/covered by data-sharing agreements, SCADA datasets in isolated Azure environments with RBAC. Missing: no mention of Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) for the processing of personal data of Ukrainian students — this is likely required under GDPR Article 35 given the high-risk context (conflict zone, potentially sensitive data). Also no mention of data retention periods or student data deletion protocols.

## Top Strengths

- Exceptionally well-evidenced needs analysis grounded in IEA, World Bank, and HEREs survey data with specific, verifiable statistics that make the curriculum gap concrete and urgent.
- Technical depth is genuine and substantial: the four course modules (DT architecture, PINNs, SCADA/IoT, cybersecurity) are described at a level of specificity — citing IEC 62443, IEC 61850, PyTorch, MATPOWER — that distinguishes this from generic digital transformation proposals and demonstrates real domain expertise.
- Wartime-adapted resilience design is genuinely novel in the European educational landscape: remote-first architecture, offline-downloadable materials, Azure local caching, asynchronous assessment, and a specific cybersecurity module addressing Industroyer-type attacks on Ukrainian grid infrastructure.
- Four-mechanism sustainability architecture (accreditation, three-generation trained faculty cohort, Living Lab as permanent institutional infrastructure, Horizon Europe follow-on) is structurally sound and addresses the most common post-project failure modes with specific institutional commitments rather than aspirational language.
- Anti-corruption, SEAH, and cross-cutting compliance sections are among the most operationally specific and credible in the proposal's peer cohort, with named mechanisms, audit commitments, and clear accountability chains.

## Critical Weaknesses

- Partner 2 is entirely unconfirmed: no institution name, no accreditation documentation, no named personnel, no signed Letter of Commitment. This is a programme eligibility requirement (minimum 2 Ukrainian HEIs) and a structural implementation risk — WP3 has no confirmed lead, 28% of the budget has no confirmed recipient, and the 180-student target is unsubstantiated for approximately half the pipeline.

- No itemised budget table is provided. The 29% Norwegian personnel cost claim, the Living Lab equipment envelope, scholarship costs, and the cost-effectiveness calculation (EUR 1,200 per student) cannot be independently verified, making sub-criterion 2.3 difficult to assess with confidence.
- No Gantt chart or visual project timeline is included for a 5-WP, 36-month project — this is a significant omission that prevents reviewers from assessing workload sequencing, resource bottlenecks, and whether the parallel demands on ZNU personnel in the critical Months 4–18 window are realistic.
