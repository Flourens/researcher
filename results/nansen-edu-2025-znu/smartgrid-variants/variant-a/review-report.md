# Review Report: AI-SmartGrid
## AI-Powered Smart Grid Education for Ukraine's Energy Resilience

**Overall Score:** 5.3/7
**Qualifies for Funding:** YES ✓

## Executive Summary

AI-SmartGrid is a well-conceived, technically sophisticated proposal that addresses a genuine and urgent need at the intersection of Ukraine's energy reconstruction crisis and the digital transformation of engineering education. The project demonstrates exceptional contextual grounding — with detailed knowledge of Zaporizhzhia's specific energy crisis, quantified workforce shortfall data, and alignment with EU Facility and Ukraine Recovery Plan priorities — and presents the strongest case for relevance among proposals in this thematic space. The NTNU–ZNU partnership is credible and built on a documented prior cooperation framework, with NTNU's National Smart Grid Laboratory and ZNU's unique frontline energy data assets constituting a genuinely complementary resource base. The five-WP structure, phased timeline, and SMART objectives (120 students, 20 staff, 6 accredited modules, 12 scholarships, 4 open datasets, 6 publications) are concrete and internally consistent.

However, the proposal carries several weaknesses that prevent it from reaching the highest scoring tier. Most critically, the second Ukrainian partner institution (Partner 2) is entirely unnamed and unconfirmed, representing a structural deficiency that violates the Nansen EDU mandatory requirement of a minimum of two Ukrainian HEIs. Approximately 25% of total budget and the WP3 co-lead role are allocated to this unknown entity, rendering those sections speculative. Key personnel throughout are listed as '[Name TBC]', which weakens the credibility of the staffing plan and makes the coordinator CV requirement impossible to verify. The budget is described in percentage terms and selective line items rather than as a complete costed breakdown, making cost-effectiveness claims difficult to validate fully. Risk treatment of the security environment, while structurally sound, relies heavily on ZNU's existing Azure infrastructure without adequately addressing what happens if ZNU's institutional continuity is itself threatened by intensified frontline operations.

The cross-cutting issues are addressed with above-average thoroughness — particularly anti-corruption, SEAH, and data protection — and the sustainability plan is one of the proposal's genuine strengths, with concrete post-project commitments from ZNU including a 0.1 FTE maintenance role and NTNU's three-year advisory commitment. Overall, the proposal is fundable and above threshold if the second Ukrainian partner is confirmed before submission, key personnel are named, and the budget is fully specified. With those corrections, this project has strong potential to be a flagship Nansen EDU investment.

## Criterion Scores

### Relevance of the project (6/7, weight: 30%)

**Sub-scores:**
- 1.1_need: 6/7
- 1.2_results: 6/7
- 1.3_ambition: 6/7
- 1.4_innovation: 6/7
- 1.5_added_value: 5/7

**Strengths:**
- Exceptionally well-documented needs analysis: precise statistics on grid damage (>80% thermal capacity destroyed), workforce shortfall (15,000+ engineers quantified by IRENA/UEA), and reconstruction financing context (EUR 50bn EU Facility) situate the project within an evidence base that is specific, sourced, and compelling.
- SMART objectives are genuinely SMART: 6 modules × 5 ECTS, 120 students (≥40% women), 20 staff (≥35% women), 12 scholarships, 2 professional short courses reaching 60 industry professionals, 4 open datasets, 6 publications, ≥2 adopting HEIs — all with month-level timelines and measurable baselines.
- Timing argument is persuasive and specific: the alignment of project duration (2026–2029) with Ukraine's projected reconstruction surge and graduate pipeline (engineers graduating 2027–2032 as primary reconstruction human capital) is analytically coherent and well-articulated.
- Thematic alignment with Nansen EDU priorities is strong: the project sits at the intersection of 'Digital transformation' and 'Environment and energy' — two explicit programme priority areas — with direct linkage to all three programme objectives (Ukrainian HEI capacity, qualified professionals, long-term cooperation).
- Four clearly articulated innovations — war-context AI datasets, frontline digital twin, hybrid-by-design architecture, and industry co-design — are substantively differentiated from generic smart grid education proposals and represent genuine methodological novelty.
- Systematic review of existing initiatives (14 Erasmus+ projects at ZNU, Horizon MSCA projects, EU4Energy, USAID Energy Security, DAAD Ukraine Digital) confirms awareness of the landscape and credibly establishes that the specific AI/smart grid niche is unoccupied.

**Weaknesses:**
- The IRENA/UEA '15,000 engineers' figure (Reference 6) and several other key statistics cite a 2023 report; the proposal would benefit from confirming these figures remain current given the pace of change in Ukraine's energy situation and potential data revisions.
- Sub-factor 1.5 (added value beyond what exists): While Erasmus+ and other programmes are reviewed at ZNU's level, the proposal does not systematically survey what comparable Ukrainian technical universities (e.g., KPI Kyiv, Kharkiv Polytechnic) are doing in AI/energy education — the gap analysis is institution-centric rather than system-wide.
- The professional short courses for industry professionals (60 engineers over 3 years) are a welcome tertiary target, but the proposal does not explain how these 60 individuals are recruited or selected, which industry partners beyond Zaporizhzhiaoblenergo and Ukrenergo will participate, or what accreditation/certification these short courses carry.
- The ambition to achieve ≥70% employment in energy/digital sectors within 12 months of graduation is presented as a target but lacks a baseline comparator: what is the current employment rate of ZNU electrical engineering graduates in relevant fields? Without this, the additionality cannot be assessed.

**Recommendations:**
- Confirm or update the IRENA/UEA 15,000-engineer shortfall figure with a 2024/2025 source, or acknowledge the projection's uncertainty range explicitly.
- Add a brief mapping of AI/energy curricula at 3–4 other major Ukrainian technical HEIs (e.g., Igor Sikorsky KPI, NTU Kharkiv Polytechnic) to demonstrate the gap is system-wide, not just a ZNU-specific absence.
- Specify the recruitment mechanism, selection criteria, and any certification/CPD recognition for the 60 industry professional short-course participants.
- Provide a baseline figure for ZNU electrical engineering graduate employment outcomes (current state) to substantiate the additionality of the ≥70% sector employment target.

### Quality of design and implementation (5/7, weight: 20%)

**Sub-scores:**
- 2.1_implementation_plan: 5/7
- 2.2_risk_assessment: 5/7
- 2.3_cost_effectiveness: 4/7

**Strengths:**
- Five-WP structure is logical, comprehensive, and correctly sequenced: Foundation → Pilots → Scaling phases mirror standard curriculum development methodology, and WP dependencies are internally consistent (WP1 module development precedes WP3 student training; WP4 Living Lab setup precedes WP3 real-data exercises).
- Equipment procurement is explicitly scheduled for completion by Month 18, which is well within the mandatory '12 months before project end' rule and provides a genuine 18-month buffer for procurement delays — demonstrating awareness of programme requirements.
- Risk assessment addresses all mandatory cross-cutting categories (security, corruption, climate, gender, human rights, SEAH, data protection, export control, academic freedom) with specific mitigations rather than boilerplate language. The three-state Security Contingency Protocol (Normal/Disrupted/Emergency) is a notably concrete operational safeguard.
- The hybrid-by-design delivery architecture is a structural risk mitigation embedded in WP1 rather than an afterthought, ensuring that security-triggered disruptions do not invalidate the core educational model.
- GDPR compliance is addressed with specificity: data-sharing agreement requirements, Azure EU Data Boundary commitment, and anonymisation protocols for grid operational data are all mentioned.
- Norwegian personnel budget at 29% of total is explicitly stated as within the 30% cap, demonstrating compliance awareness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The complete budget breakdown is not presented: only percentage allocations by partner (NTNU 25%, ZNU 45%, Partner 2 25%, admin 5%) and selective line items (NOK 200,000 hardware, NOK 80,000 software, EUR 30,000 scholarships, NOK 1,450,000 Norwegian personnel) are given. A full cost category breakdown (personnel, procurement, travel, scholarships, other) against the NOK 5,000,000 total is needed to verify cost-effectiveness claims.
- Partner 2 receives approximately 25% of the budget (approximately NOK 1,250,000) but is entirely unnamed and unconfirmed. Budget allocation to an unidentified partner is a significant planning gap that prevents meaningful assessment of value for money for that portion.
- The security risk section, while strong conceptually, does not address the scenario of ZNU's institutional continuity being severely disrupted — i.e., if ZNU as an institution is forced to fully evacuate or suspend operations for an extended period (not merely campus inaccessibility). There is no identified backup Ukrainian institutional host.
- The risk matrix format is narrative rather than quantified: while probability/impact qualitative labels are given (High/Medium/Low), there is no residual risk assessment after mitigations or a quantified risk register that would allow prioritisation.
- The timeline summary table covers only 12 milestones; a full Gantt chart or activity-level timeline (which would normally be in an annex) is absent from the narrative, making it difficult to verify resource loading across months.
- Cost-effectiveness sub-criterion: the claim that Norwegian personnel costs are NOK 1,450,000 (29%) implies the total project budget is approximately NOK 5,000,000, but this is not confirmed explicitly. The arithmetic should be stated openly.

**Recommendations:**
- Include a complete budget table showing all cost categories (personnel by partner, equipment, software, travel, scholarships, other) against the NOK 5,000,000 total — even if at the level of budget lines rather than individual invoices.
- Name and confirm Partner 2 before submission, or include a contingency provision specifying what happens to the 25% Partner 2 budget and WP3 co-lead role if Partner 2 is not confirmed — including whether ZNU could absorb those activities.
- Add a scenario analysis for ZNU institutional suspension: identify a designated alternative Ukrainian HEI partner or a plan to shift all Ukrainian activities to fully remote/cloud delivery managed by ZNU staff from wherever they are relocated.
- Develop a formal risk register (even a simple 5×5 matrix) with pre- and post-mitigation residual risk ratings for each identified risk category.
- Provide a month-by-month Gantt chart as an annex, showing all WP activities, milestones, personnel deployment, and exchange visits.

### Quality of project group (4/7, weight: 20%)

**Sub-scores:**
- 3.1_complementarity: 5/7
- 3.2_task_division: 4/7
- 3.3_interaction_plan: 5/7

**Strengths:**
- NTNU–ZNU complementarity is genuinely strong and well-articulated: NTNU provides AI/power systems research excellence, tested curriculum frameworks, and National Smart Grid Laboratory infrastructure; ZNU provides contextual Ukrainian grid knowledge, HPC and Azure infrastructure, real solar generation data, and industry relationships with Zaporizhzhia energy utilities. These are non-overlapping and mutually reinforcing assets.
- The governance structure is clearly specified: Steering Committee (quarterly, online) with defined composition, decision rules (consensus, NTNU casting vote), and secretariat support; WP lead/co-lead pairs from different institutions for each WP; monthly bilateral calls; shared Teams workspace.
- Stakeholder engagement plan is unusually detailed for this proposal type: Zaporizhzhiaoblenergo and Ukrenergo are engaged not just as advisors but as data providers and industry panellists, with formal data-sharing agreements by M4; Ministry of Education and Science engagement is planned with a specific deliverable (curriculum standard endorsement submission at M24); student representation in WP3 coordination calls is explicitly included.
- The allocation of WP leads reflects a genuine capacity-building intent: ZNU leads WP2 (Staff Training), WP3 (Student Programme), and WP4 (Living Lab), ensuring that the Ukrainian partner holds primary responsibility for the core educational delivery activities rather than being a passive recipient of Norwegian expertise.
- No critical expertise area is covered by a single person — the proposal explicitly notes redundancy across the team, reducing key-person dependency risk.

**Weaknesses:**
- The most significant weakness under this criterion: Partner 2 is entirely unnamed. This means the minimum requirement of two Ukrainian partner HEIs is currently unmet on paper. Partner 2's key personnel (WP3 co-lead, WP1 contributor) are 'TBC', making it impossible to assess their complementarity, expertise, or genuine commitment. Letters of Commitment from all partners are a mandatory submission requirement; a letter from an unnamed institution cannot exist.
- All NTNU key personnel are listed as '[Name TBC]' including the Project Coordinator, whose CV is a mandatory application document (maximum 4 pages). This is a disqualifying administrative gap if not resolved before submission.
- The description of Partner 2's budget share (25%, approximately NOK 1,250,000) and role (WP3 co-lead, 40 students, WP1 contributor) is detailed enough to suggest this partner has been identified but is being withheld — if so, naming them now would significantly strengthen the proposal.
- The 5% indirect/admin cost allocation described as 'covered proportionally by each institution within their national overhead rates' is vague: it is unclear whether this means institutional overhead is included in the partner percentages or is an additional allocation, and whether it complies with Nansen EDU eligible cost categories.
- SINTEF Energy Research is mentioned as a contributing partner ('no separate budget line required') but without any formal commitment letter or defined deliverable — if SINTEF's contribution is substantive to the Living Lab technical advisory, its participation should be formalised.

**Recommendations:**
- Name Partner 2 in the final application and provide a signed Letter of Commitment. If Partner 2 is genuinely in consultation, accelerate confirmation — this is the single most critical fix required for eligibility.
- Name all key personnel and attach the Project Coordinator's CV (max 4 pages) as a mandatory document. Even if final contractual arrangements are pending, named individuals with brief CVs are expected in the proposal body.
- Clarify SINTEF Energy Research's role: either formalise it with a contribution description and Letter of Commitment (if substantive), or remove the reference to avoid creating an expectation of involvement that is not backed by a commitment.
- Specify the indirect cost treatment: state explicitly whether the 5% admin figure is included within or additional to the partner budget allocations, and confirm compliance with Nansen EDU eligible cost rules.
- Consider adding a brief section explaining how Partner 2 was/will be selected — what criteria determined that this institution is the right second partner — to demonstrate strategic rationale for the consortium composition.

### Project impact (5/7, weight: 30%)

**Sub-scores:**
- 4.1_impact: 6/7
- 4.2_sustainability: 5/7
- 4.3_evaluation: 5/7

**Strengths:**
- Impact pathway is unusually well-specified: the chain from 120 directly trained students → 3,600 person-years of specialised human capital → quantified contribution to closing the 15,000-engineer gap is analytically robust, and the multiplicative reach through ≥2 adopting HEIs (estimated 200+ students/year at scale) demonstrates genuine systemic ambition.
- Sustainability plan is one of the proposal's genuine highlights: curriculum accreditation embeds modules into regular degree programmes (not project-funded silos); ZNU commits to a 0.1 FTE technical engineer position for Living Lab operations from Year 3 institutional budget; NTNU commits to a 3-year post-project advisory relationship formalised in a Memorandum of Cooperation; open-source tools eliminate recurring licence costs; Azure costs folded into existing institutional subscription.
- Evaluation framework is multi-level and methodologically sound: output level (pre/post competency assessment using IEEE CSDP framework), process level (satisfaction surveys with 2-week corrective action cycle), impact level (12-month alumni employment tracking), and sustainability level (annual curriculum adoption survey through M48 post-project) address all four OECD-DAC evaluation tiers.
- Institutional impact on ZNU is well-articulated: the Living Lab as a permanent infrastructure creates a platform for future Horizon Europe applications and industry research contracts, giving ZNU a new institutional identity axis beyond the project period.
- Dissemination plan is specific and credible: named target journals (IEEE Transactions on Smart Grid, IEEE Transactions on Power Systems, Energies MDPI), named conference venues (IEEE PowerTech 2027 Grenoble, CIGRE 2028 Paris, Ukrainian Energy Forum 2027), and CC BY 4.0 open licensing on ZNU institutional repository and OpenUkraineEdu platform.
- The 60 industry professionals reached through short courses represent a near-term operational impact pathway for project outputs, particularly valuable given the reconstruction surge expected from 2026.

**Weaknesses:**
- The alumni employment tracking mechanism (12-month post-graduation survey via ZNU's career services office) is sensible but relies on ZNU having maintained contact with graduates — in a war-affected context with significant student displacement and mobility, graduate contact data may be difficult to maintain. No fallback data collection method is described.
- The Horizon Europe follow-on funding plan targets 'HORIZON-WIDERA-2027 Twinning or CL5-2027 Energy call' but these are distinct instruments with different eligibility, consortium requirements, and project logics. The proposal does not demonstrate which is more appropriate or whether preliminary Horizon contacts have been initiated — the Letter of Intent target (M30) may be too late to allow meaningful Horizon 2027 preparation.
- The ≥2 additional Ukrainian HEI adoption target is ambitious but the mechanism is underspecified: outreach to '5 Ukrainian universities' in WP5 is mentioned, but there is no description of the incentive structure or support for adoption. CC BY licensing is necessary but not sufficient — adopting institutions will need faculty training and technical support that the project budget does not explicitly provide for.
- Impact on NTNU is presented (research data access, policy profile) but not quantified — while this is less important than Ukrainian impact, some indication of expected publications or student outcomes at NTNU would strengthen the bilateral impact case.
- The 6-month pre-graduation alumni survey is listed alongside the 12-month survey but 6 months post-graduation is too early for meaningful employment data in engineering careers; this data point adds little analytical value.
- Post-project curriculum update mechanism: NTNU's '1–2 online consultations per year' for 3 years is very light for a 6-module curriculum in a fast-moving field (AI and smart grid technology). Without a more structured update protocol, module content risks becoming outdated within 2–3 years of project end.

**Recommendations:**
- Develop a backup alumni tracking strategy for conflict-affected contexts: e.g., LinkedIn group for cohort graduates, partnership with ZNU's student union for contact maintenance, or integration with Ukrainian government student registration systems.
- Clarify the Horizon Europe follow-on funding strategy: decide between WIDERA Twinning and CL5 Energy, initiate preliminary partner mapping now, and set an earlier internal milestone (M20) for Horizon consortium formation rather than waiting until M30 for a Letter of Intent.
- Design a light-touch faculty development package (e.g., a 2-day online orientation workshop) specifically for adopting HEIs as part of WP5, with a modest budget line, to make adoption practically achievable rather than aspirational.
- Strengthen the post-project module update protocol: specify a more structured annual curriculum review process involving at least one technical expert (e.g., from NTNU or IEEE Smart Grid community) to keep content current beyond project end.
- Replace or supplement the 6-month alumni survey with a more meaningful early-career tracking metric (e.g., graduate thesis/project topic as a proxy for competency application) if 6-month employment data proves unreliable in the Ukrainian context.

## Cross-Cutting Compliance

- **antiCorruption:** ✓ — Strong. Ukraine's CPI score (35/100) is explicitly cited and the risk is rated Medium probability/High impact. Four specific mitigations are described: all funds managed through NTNU's institutional financial system with no cash payments in Ukraine; reimbursement against documented invoices; competitive tendering for contracts above NOK 100,000; joint NTNU-ZNU scholarship selection committee with documented criteria and direct bank disbursement. ZNU's 14 Erasmus+/3 MSCA project track record is cited as evidence of prior EU-standard financial compliance. This is one of the better anti-corruption treatments seen in proposals of this type.
- **climate:** ✓ — Adequate but somewhat formulaic. The proposal correctly identifies the substantive contribution (training engineers in renewable energy integration directly supports Ukraine's NDC 25% renewable target) and specifies operational measures (minimising air travel through 5–10 day combined visits, online-first meetings, EU Energy Label A hardware, 7-year equipment lifespan). However, no carbon footprint estimate is provided for the 20 staff exchange visits to/from Ukraine and 12 student mobility trips — these are the most significant emission sources and their climate impact is not quantified or compared against benefits.
- **gender:** ✓ — Good, with genuine structural embedding rather than aspirational statements. Quantified targets (≥40% women students, ≥35% women staff) are designated as KPIs tracked in quarterly SC reporting with a documented corrective action process. ZNU co-project lead position is designated for a woman researcher. Asynchronous module delivery accommodates caregiving burdens. Inclusive language in recruitment materials is specified in both English and Ukrainian. Weakness: the proposal does not describe a gender disaggregated analysis of current ZNU student enrolment in relevant programmes to establish whether the 40% target is realistic or aspirational given a 22% baseline female graduation rate in Ukrainian electrical engineering.
- **humanRights:** ✓ — Adequate. The assessment addresses non-discrimination (merit-based criteria, materials available in Ukrainian and Russian for displaced learners), accountability (anonymous grievance mechanism via project website from Month 1), and participation (student representatives in curriculum review panels). Right to education (UDHR Article 26) and right to work are explicitly cited. However, the assessment is brief and does not address specific vulnerabilities of internally displaced persons as a distinct group, potential language barriers for Russian-speaking displaced students in an increasingly Ukrainian-language environment, or the accessibility needs of students with conflict-related disabilities — all of which are relevant to the Zaporizhzhia context.
- **seah:** ✓ — Strong and operationally specific. A project-specific Code of Conduct signed by all participants at enrolment, with explicit SEAH definition and reporting procedures. Three named SEAH contact persons (one per institution) visible to all participants from the outset. Exchange visit safeguards through NTNU's International Students Office with independent counselling services. Online platform moderation policies preventing private channel isolation. Annual SEAH awareness training integrated into WP2. This treatment is above average for proposals of this type and demonstrates institutional seriousness rather than checkbox compliance.
- **dataProtection:** ✓ — Good. Three data categories are identified (student personal data, grid operational data, project financial data). GDPR compliance through NTNU's framework is stated. Grid data anonymisation and aggregation before student use is specified, with data-sharing agreements reviewed by both institutions' legal offices. Azure EU Data Boundary commitment is cited for data residency. Export control is assessed for all software tools (TensorFlow, PyTorch, DIgSILENT — confirmed as not dual-use controlled). ZNU security officer review of publications and datasets before release is explicitly included. Minor gap: no Data Management Plan is mentioned, and the academic freedom dimension (while touched on in the security review process) is not separately elaborated as required by the programme's explicit academic freedom/export control cross-cutting requirement.

- Exceptional contextual grounding and needs analysis: the proposal demonstrates authoritative knowledge of Zaporizhzhia's specific energy crisis, the Ukrainian workforce gap, EU/Ukraine reconstruction financing mechanisms, and the timing imperative — all backed by primary sources. This is well above average for educational cooperation proposals.
- Technically coherent and innovative project design: war-context AI datasets, frontline digital twin, hybrid-by-design delivery, and industry co-design are four substantive innovations that are methodologically novel and directly relevant to the reconstruction context — not superficial differentiation.
- Genuinely complementary NTNU–ZNU partnership: NTNU's National Smart Grid Laboratory and AI/power systems research excellence combine with ZNU's real solar generation data, HPC infrastructure, wartime operational experience, and Zaporizhzhia industry relationships in a way that creates mutual benefit and cannot be replicated by either partner alone.
- Strong sustainability architecture: curriculum accreditation, ZNU's institutional commitment to a 0.1 FTE Living Lab maintenance role, NTNU's 3-year post-project advisory MoC, open-source tool selection, and CC BY 4.0 licensing collectively give the project outputs a credible path to self-sustaining operation beyond project end.
- Comprehensive cross-cutting issue treatment: anti-corruption, SEAH, gender, and data protection are all addressed with operational specificity (named contact persons, documented processes, KPIs with corrective action triggers) rather than aspirational language — this is a genuine programme compliance strength.

## Critical Weaknesses

- Partner 2 is entirely unnamed and unconfirmed: approximately 25% of the budget, the WP3 co-lead role, and 40 of 120 student training places are allocated to an unidentified institution. This is a potential disqualifying eligibility gap (minimum 2 Ukrainian HEIs required) and must be resolved before submission. A Letter of Commitment from an unnamed partner cannot be submitted.
- All key NTNU personnel are listed as '[Name TBC]', including the Project Coordinator whose CV (max 4 pages) is a mandatory application document. An application without a named coordinator and attached CV is administratively incomplete and cannot be assessed under sub-criterion 3.1.

The cost-effectiveness assessment cannot be fully validated.
- The academic freedom cross-cutting requirement, while briefly mentioned in the data protection section (ZNU security officer review of publications), is not explicitly addressed as a standalone requirement — export control and dual-use considerations are mentioned only for software tools, without addressing whether any AI methodologies or grid topology datasets might have dual-use implications requiring more formal export control assessment.
